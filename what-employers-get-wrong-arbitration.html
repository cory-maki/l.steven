What Employers Get Wrong About Arbitration Agreements

📅 April 2026
✎ L. Steven Platt
⌚ 6 min read

An arbitration agreement is only as good as its weakest clause — and I keep seeing the same weak clauses.

Employers adopt mandatory arbitration for good reasons: faster resolution, lower cost, and privacy compared to a jury trial. But an agreement drafted without attention to current law can end up worse than no agreement at all, handing a plaintiff's lawyer an easy argument that the whole thing is unenforceable — a recurring theme across his broader employment litigation practice.

The Class Action Waiver Problem

Class and collective action waivers remain enforceable in most contexts, but the drafting has to be precise. Ambiguous language about whether claims must be arbitrated individually gives arbitrators and courts room to read the agreement against the employer. If the goal is to prevent class arbitration, the agreement needs to say so unambiguously — silence is not a strategy.

It's also worth remembering that certain claims, including sexual harassment and sexual assault claims under recent federal legislation, can no longer be forced into arbitration regardless of what the agreement says. An arbitration provision that doesn't carve those claims out correctly invites a fight over the enforceability of the entire agreement rather than just the excluded claim.

Silence is not a strategy when it comes to class action waivers.

Procedural Fairness Isn't Optional

Courts scrutinize whether an arbitration agreement is procedurally fair — adequate notice, a real opportunity to review the terms, and rules that don't stack the deck against the employee. Agreements that require the employee to pay excessive fees, that limit remedies available in court, or that impose an unreasonably short limitations period are the ones most likely to be struck down or sent back for revision.

I advise clients to use a reputable arbitration provider's published employment rules rather than drafting bespoke procedures from scratch. Courts are familiar with those rules, and using them removes an entire category of fairness challenges before they start.

Getting Employees to Actually Agree

How an agreement is presented matters as much as what it says. Burying an arbitration clause in a handbook that an employee never separately signs is a common and avoidable mistake. A standalone agreement, presented clearly, signed (or affirmatively acknowledged electronically) as a condition of employment, with a reasonable window to review it, holds up far better than a clause tucked into onboarding paperwork.

For existing employees, adding a new arbitration requirement raises separate consideration questions depending on the state. In some jurisdictions, continued employment alone is enough consideration; in others, employers need to offer something additional, like a signing bonus or updated benefits, to make the new terms enforceable, a distinction that matters just as much as the current state of non-compete enforcement discussed above.

Conclusion

Arbitration agreements can still deliver real advantages for employers, but only when they're drafted with current law in mind and rolled out in a way that will survive a fairness challenge. A periodic review, the same way you'd review any other standard employment document, is the cheapest insurance available against losing the benefit of arbitration when you need it most.
