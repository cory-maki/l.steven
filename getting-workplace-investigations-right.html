Getting Workplace Investigations Right

📅 May 2026
✎ L. Steven Platt
⌚ 7 min read

A poorly run workplace investigation can turn a manageable personnel issue into a lawsuit — sometimes two.

I've been brought in after the fact more times than I can count, to defend a company whose investigation created as much liability as the underlying complaint. The good news is that the mistakes are avoidable, and they tend to repeat themselves across industries: no clear process, an investigator with an obvious conflict of interest, or a rush to a conclusion before the facts are actually gathered — patterns I see often in his work advising employers on workplace disputes.

Start With Who's Doing the Investigating

The single biggest predictor of whether an investigation holds up is who conducts it. A direct supervisor of either party, or an HR generalist with a personal relationship to someone involved, should not be running the interviews. In higher-stakes matters — harassment allegations against a senior leader, for example — outside counsel or an independent investigator is worth the cost, because a credibility fight over the investigator can eclipse the credibility fight over the original complaint.

Document the decision about who investigates, not just the investigation itself. If a plaintiff's lawyer later argues the process was rigged, you want a contemporaneous record showing why that investigator was chosen.

Interview Technique Matters More Than People Think

Open-ended, non-leading questions produce better evidence and better outcomes. Asking a witness to confirm a version of events you've already decided on doesn't just risk missing the truth — it creates a transcript that looks coached if the matter ever gets to a deposition. I tell clients to interview the complainant, the accused, and any witnesses separately, in a logical order, and to take detailed contemporaneous notes rather than relying on memory afterward.

Credibility determinations are unavoidable in most investigations — two people rarely describe the same incident identically. Write down the reasoning, not just the conclusion. A finding that says only “we found the complaint not substantiated” is far weaker in litigation than one that explains what evidence was weighed and why.

Closing the Loop Without Creating New Risk

Employers often want to tell the complainant everything that happened as a result of the investigation. Resist that instinct. Confidentiality obligations to the accused, and legitimate personnel-privacy concerns, usually mean the complainant gets confirmation that the matter was investigated and addressed — not a play-by-play of any discipline imposed.

Finally, remedial action should match the findings and be consistent with how similar conduct has been handled before. Inconsistent discipline for comparable conduct is one of the more common threads I see in retaliation and discrimination claims that follow an investigation, much like the fairness problems that surface in poorly drafted arbitration agreements.

Conclusion

An investigation doesn't need to be perfect to hold up — it needs to be defensible. That means a credible investigator, a fair process, documented reasoning, and consistent follow-through. Employers who build that discipline into their process before they need it are in a far better position when a complaint does come in.
