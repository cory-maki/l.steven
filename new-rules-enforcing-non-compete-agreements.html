The New Rules for Enforcing Non-Compete Agreements

📅 June 2026
✎ L. Steven Platt
⌚ 6 min read

Non-compete agreements used to be one of the more predictable tools in an employer's toolkit. That predictability is gone.

Over the past several years, state legislatures, courts, and federal regulators have all moved — sometimes in different directions — on how far an employer can go to restrict where a former employee works next. Some states have banned non-competes outright for most workers. Others have narrowed them to high-earners or added strict notice and consideration requirements. The result is a patchwork that trips up even sophisticated national employers.

Why Old Templates Don't Hold Up Anymore

I still see employers relying on a non-compete template that was drafted a decade ago and never revisited. That's a mistake. A restriction that was enforceable in 2016 may be void on its face today, and a court that finds one paragraph overbroad won't always sever it — in some jurisdictions, an unenforceable provision can void the entire agreement.

The safer approach is to treat non-competes the way you'd treat any other compliance obligation: reviewed on a schedule, tailored to the state where the employee actually works, and narrow enough that a judge doesn't have to squint to see the legitimate business interest behind it.

What Courts Are Actually Looking For

In the litigation I handle, the agreements that survive a motion to dismiss or a preliminary injunction hearing share a few traits. The geographic and time restrictions are tied to where the business actually competes, not a nationwide, multi-year default. The role-specific language reflects what the employee actually did, not a boilerplate list of job functions. And the employer can point to a specific, provable interest — client relationships, trade secrets, specialized training — rather than a general desire to slow down a competitor.

Courts have also gotten more skeptical of non-competes used on lower-wage or entry-level employees, where the legitimate-interest argument is weakest. If your agreement can't survive the question “what exactly are you protecting here,” it's worth rewriting before you need to enforce it — a review I regularly walk clients through as part of his four decades defending employers in labor and employment disputes.

Practical Steps for Employers

Start with an inventory: which employees actually have restrictive covenants, in which states, and under which version of the agreement. Most companies are surprised by what they find. From there, prioritize the roles where a departure would genuinely hurt the business, and build restrictions around those roles specifically rather than applying one form to everyone — the same discipline that goes into conducting workplace investigations that hold up under scrutiny.

It also pays to think about enforcement before you need it. An employer who has never enforced a non-compete has a harder time convincing a court the interest is real. Consistent, documented enforcement — even something as simple as a firm cease-and-desist letter — matters when a judge is weighing whether to grant an injunction.

Conclusion

Non-compete law will keep shifting as more states weigh in and federal regulators continue to take an interest. Employers who treat their agreements as a living compliance document, rather than something drafted once and forgotten, are the ones who end up with restrictions that actually hold up when it matters.
